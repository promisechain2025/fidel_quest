Reviewer's Guide · v1

Fidel Quest, feature by feature

🦁 A walkthrough of everything in the app — with an honest opinion on each part. Play through it, then tell us what you think at the bottom.

← Back to the game

Fidel Quest teaches the Fidel — the Amharic & Tigrinya alphabet (231 letters across 33 families × 7 vowel orders) — to young children through play. It's a free, install-free web game with a lion cub named Anbessa, a star friend Kokeb, and a mischievous hyena Jibby. No accounts, no ads, nothing leaves the device.

Before you start reviewing

  • 1Open the game in a private / incognito window the first time — that guarantees you see the newest version, not an old cached one.
  • 2Turn the sound on (speaker button, lower-left) and use headphones or a decent speaker — the letters are real human recordings.
  • 3When you try Say-it, the browser will ask for the microphone — allow it. Nothing is recorded or uploaded; it's analysed on the device and thrown away.
  • 4Set your language in Backpack → App text (8 options) and the letters to learn under Learning (Amharic or Tigrinya).
  • 5Best audience: roughly ages 3–8, plus heritage learners of any age.

The Home Journey

The main screen — a winding path you climb one step at a time.

The winding path

Home screen

Solid

A single trail of stepping-stones. Exactly one stone pulses — your next step. Letters, mixed reviews, boss quizzes and arcade games unlock strictly in order, so a child is never lost or overwhelmed by choice.

Try it — Tap the one glowing stone. Finish it. Watch the next stone light up and Anbessa collect a reward.

Honest take
  • +The "one obvious next thing" design is genuinely calming and motivating (the Duolingo lesson-path idea, done well).
  • +Progress is saved on the device automatically — close and come back, it's all there.
  • –The full path is ~74 steps, so it's a very long scroll. There's no "jump to my current step" button yet, which can feel daunting on a small phone.
✨

Letter Steps — the five mini-games

Each new letter-family is taught through the same five playful phases, learn-first.

1 · Bubble Pop

First phase of every Letter Step

Polished

A big, glossy, dancing bubble drifts around carrying the new letter. Tap it to hear the sound. The letters are bright white on vivid colour balls — deliberately high-contrast so they're easy to see (and colour-blind-friendly).

Try it — Pop the bubble a few times. Say the sound back to Anbessa each pop.

Honest take
  • +Inviting, tactile first contact; the colour + motion + contrast work were done carefully.
  • –It's purely receptive (listen only) — no challenge. That's intentional for a first meeting, but don't expect it to test anything.

2 · Star Trail & 3 · Feed Anbessa

Middle phases

Solid

Star Trail traces the family's letters like a constellation. Feed Anbessa is call-and-response: you hear a letter and tap the matching "cookie" to feed the lion. There's a “Hear it again” button, and a wrong tap gives a gentle second chance rather than punishing.

Try it — Listen, then feed Anbessa the letter you heard. Miss on purpose once to see the "try again" kindness.

Honest take
  • +The character warmth (feeding the cub) makes drill feel like care, not testing.
  • –Can get repetitive across many families; the star-trail phase is more decorative than instructive.

4 · Jibby Shuffle

Feed-before-the-hyena phase

Solid

Same feeding, now with a little urgency — feed the right letter before Jibby the hyena snatches it. Introduces a villain and light time-pressure without being scary.

Try it — Beat Jibby to the letter. Use "Hear it again" if you miss it the first time.

Honest take
  • +Jibby is a great low-stakes antagonist; the tension lifts engagement.
  • –The timing may stress very young or anxious children — worth watching.

5 · Trace & carve (writing)

Final phase of a family

Promising

The child now writes. It says a letter, then they trace it with a finger — three real letters of the family in a row (with a 1/3 progress counter and a "hear it" button). The pen is chunky and the scoring is forgiving so a genuine attempt succeeds.

Try it — Trace over the grey letter. Cover most of it and tap Check.

Honest take
  • +Sound-first, multi-letter, and it never blocks a child with harsh grading — the right call for confidence.
  • –There's no true stroke-order teaching (Ge'ez has no public stroke dataset), so it scores how much you covered, not how you drew it — a determined scribble can pass.
🎯

Boss Quizzes

The "listen and pick" checkpoints between chapters.

“Which letter says…?”

Quiz boss stones

Polished

Hear a sound, tap the matching letter from a few choices. It advances by itself after a correct answer (no "Continue" button), a wrong answer lets you try again, and letters you miss quietly come back more often later.

Try it — Answer a few. Notice it moves on on its own, and never shows two letters that sound the same.

Honest take
  • +Clean, fast, forgiving; the adaptive "missed letters resurface" is real spaced-practice, not decoration.
  • –A simple format by design — it leans entirely on the audio being clear.
🏛️

The Arcade — Anbessa & Jibby in 3D

The reward games, set in real Ethiopian landmarks.

Letter Runner

Arcade gateway stones

Solid

A 3D lane-runner through Lalibela, Aksum, Gondar and more. Hear a letter and steer Anbessa into the right lane; Jibby chases when you miss. You can pick a speed (slow / normal / fast), and — importantly — it only ever asks about letters the child has already learned.

Try it — Set the speed to slow, then steer Anbessa into the letter you hear. Speed up when it feels easy.

Honest take
  • +Genuinely fun, and the landmarks quietly teach geography/heritage. "Only quiz what they've learned" is exactly right.
  • +Falls back to a 2D version automatically on slower devices, so it doesn't just freeze.
  • –The letters can still read a little far at a glance; and 3D is the heaviest thing in the app.

Fidel Skylands

Some arcade stones

Solid

A floating-island 3D mode with a cumulative quiz and a Jibby boss round that reviews everything so far.

Try it — Play through an island; the boss at the end mixes earlier letters back in.

Honest take
  • +Good cumulative review wrapped in spectacle.
  • –Mechanically similar to the runner quiz; mostly a change of scenery.

The Backpack — the toolkit

Tap the backpack (top-right). Everything here is free-play, off the main path.

Letter Explorer

Backpack → Letter Explorer

Polished

Every one of the 33 families as a colourful tile — tap to hear it, open one to see all seven vowel forms, and use the vowel picker to see the same vowel across every family at once.

Try it — Pick a vowel (e.g. "Rab' · -aa") and hear how every family changes with it.

Honest take
  • +The best free-reference in the app; the vowel picker makes the abugida's structure click.
  • –It's a reference, not a game — no goal or reward.

First Words

Backpack → First Words

Solid

Hear a whole word and tap its picture (ልጅ = child 👶, ማር = honey 🍯). Sometimes it shows the picture and asks which letter the word starts with — the one place look-alike "twin" letters appear together, told apart by meaning.

Try it — Hear the word, tap the matching picture.

Honest take
  • +Connects letters to real, kid-familiar words — the leap from sounds to reading.
  • –The word list is still small (only some families have a word yet, and a few await a recording). More words are planned.

Star Practice

Backpack (appears when you've missed letters)

Solid

A short remedial round built only from the exact letters the child has gotten wrong — pure targeted practice.

Honest take
  • +Smart, personal, and it never touches your stars/score — pressure-free.
  • –Only shows up once there's something to practise, so new players won't see it.

Anbessa's Closet & Share

Backpack → Anbessa's Closet (or tap the lion)

Polished

Dress Anbessa in hats, scarves and capes earned by finishing steps, then tap Share Anbessa to make a card ("X / 231 letters learned") for WhatsApp/social — the app's word-of-mouth loop.

Try it — Put a cape on Anbessa, then Share to see the card it makes.

Honest take
  • +The reward that kids actually want; the share card is a clean, private growth loop (nothing leaves the device unless the parent shares).
  • –Cosmetic — no bearing on learning. The card image quality depends on the device.

Anbessa Tee Shop

Backpack → Anbessa Tee Shop

Promising

Each chapter mastered unlocks a wearable T-shirt design showing Anbessa in the child's earned outfit + their progress. A parent can save a print-ready image or tap "Order a real shirt."

Try it — Open a design and tap "Save the design" to see the print-ready picture.

Honest take
  • +A genuinely clever idea — turn the motivation the game already creates into keepsakes / a small income stream.
  • –It's a scaffold, not a live store: "Order" only opens a shop once one is configured; otherwise it just saves the PNG.

Classic Game

Backpack → Classic Game

Solid

The original game, preserved: seven levels, an Explore Mode that chants a whole letter-row aloud (with slow/normal/fast pace), and a Tracing mode.

Try it — Explore Mode → open a family → "Chant the row," and slow the pace down.

Honest take
  • +The chant is lovely and true to how the Fidel is taught in classrooms.
  • –It overlaps with the main Journey and Fidel Master — three places to "hear letters" is a little redundant.

For Grown-Ups

Backpack → For grown-ups

Solid

A small dashboard of progress and the letters a child finds tricky.

Honest take
  • +Exactly the at-a-glance a parent wants.
  • –Still shows in English under some of the newer languages.

Fidel Master — the mastery trainer

Reached from Classic Game → Fidel Master. Three tools for drilling the whole alphabet.

Chart · Auto-voice · Say-it

Classic Game → Fidel Master

Promising

Chart is a bright, tappable abugida with a "Play the whole Abugida" button. Auto-voice reads letters aloud continuously — pick a single vowel or the whole set, a "Say with me" pause so kids chant along, and a pace control. Say-it shows a letter, the child speaks into the mic, and it gives encouraging feedback.

Try it — Auto-voice tab → pick one vowel → turn on "Say with me" → set it slow. Then try Say-it and speak a letter.

Honest take
  • +Chart and Auto-voice are lovely and genuinely useful for mastery + chant-along practice.
  • –Be clear about Say-it: it's private/offline by design, so it can tell that the child spoke and roughly how long — it does not truly judge whether the Amharic sound was correct. It's encouragement, not a pronunciation score (a real score needs cloud speech recognition, deliberately not used to protect a child's voice).

Languages, Audio & Under the Hood

Two alphabets, eight app languages

Backpack → Learning / App text

Solid

Learn the Amharic or Tigrinya letters; read the app itself in English, Amharic, German, Italian, Swedish, Dutch, Norwegian or French — chosen for where Ethiopian & Eritrean families live. Even the praise words ("Bravo!", "Bra jobbat!") follow your language.

Honest take
  • +Rare thoughtfulness for a diaspora audience; graceful — anything untranslated simply shows in English.
  • –The newer translations are best-effort and want a native-speaker pass.

Real human voice (with one gap)

Everywhere

One recording missing

Every letter is a real human recording — no robotic text-to-speech anywhere. A gentle musical chime stands in only when a clip is genuinely missing, and there's a soft hand-clap when you win.

Honest take
  • +The human voice is the app's backbone and it sounds warm and clear.
  • –The Tigrinya "ሐ" (ḥ) has no distinct recording yet, so on Tigrinya it borrows the Amharic "h" — audibly wrong to a Tigrinya ear. It needs one short recording to fix. (A couple of words and the spoken praise words are also awaiting recordings.)

Installable, offline, private

Add to Home Screen

Polished

Add it to a phone's home screen and it launches full-screen like a real app, works with no internet after the first load, needs no account, and keeps all progress on the device — nothing is collected.

Honest take
  • +A privacy stance most kids' apps don't come close to — genuinely offline and account-free.
  • –The very first load needs a connection (to fetch the audio and font).

The honest bottom line

A warm, thoughtful, genuinely playable alphabet game — with a handful of honest gaps.

The core loop (learn a letter → play with it → write it → get quizzed → earn a reward) is well-built and kind to children: it never punishes, it adapts to what they miss, and it's wrapped in real characters and real places. The privacy and offline stance is exemplary, and the diaspora-language support is unusually caring.

The weak spots are honest and fixable: Say-it can't truly grade pronunciation (on purpose), Tigrinya ḥ and more first-words need recordings, the newer translations want a native check, there's some overlap between Classic / Fidel Master / Explorer, and the home path is a long scroll. None of these break the experience — but a reviewer should know them.
